Caroline Fry, "Building Bridges: The impact of return migration by African scientists"

The third installment in this series is Caroline Fry‚Äôs paper, ‚ÄúBuilding Bridges: The impact of migration by African scientists.‚Äù Here‚Äôs an excerpt from the abstract:

Despite significant interest in the potential for ‚Äòreturnee‚Äô scientists moving back to developing countries to connect developed and developing countries, prior work has found limited evidence of success. I shift the focus to the broader network of the returnee, and study the extent to which the return home of American-trained HIV researchers to African institutions impacts publication outcomes of non-migrant scientists in Africa. I find that following the arrival of a returnee in their institution, non-migrants experience increased productivity, mostly in HIV research. I find strong evidence that the mechanism driving this effect is that of the returnee providing a bridge to their central connections and subsequent knowledge and resources thus affecting outcomes.

Research questions on the effects of networks are hard to translate to clean causal inference questions. First of all, networks aren‚Äôt formed randomly: we select ourselves and are selected into networks. Second, it can be difficult to decipher what exactly is the effect produced from purely being in the network group as opposed to, say, the effect of some background characteristic shared by members of the group. This problem is called Manski‚Äôs reflection problem. For instance, if the univeristy was recently endowned with funding‚Äìan exogenous shock‚Äìthere might be an increase in publication rate overall across the university separate from being in a network of a US-trained researcher. In my review, I will discuss whether and how Fry overcomes these challenges in her research design.

Research question

The research question is, ‚ÄúWhen HIV researchers return to African institutions after their training in American institutions, what is the effect on the publication outcomes of local scientists?‚Äù The training refers to the AIDS International Training and Research Program (AITRP) funded by National Institute of Health (NIH) Fogarty International Center (FIC). Research universities in the United States that participate in this program are given grants to design a training program related to research on HIV and AIDS. What is interesting is that FIC provides arrangements to encourage the trainees, who are selected from low and middle income countries, to return home (11-12). Therefore, the treatment event is these trainees‚Äô return to their home countries.

The author wants to show that local scientists working at the institutions that receive a trainee benefits from the knowledge and connection that she brings. This is a little bit different from the outcomes studied in the existing literature where the focus was on the trainees themselves, whose productivity, according to these studies, dropped after their return from abroad. Thus the existing literature has been pessimistic about the benefits of networking. However, Fry argues that we‚Äôre looking at the wrong place.üîç According to the theory of second-hand brokerage, between the core (the United States) and the periphery (African countries) are brokers who bridge the two clusters (see Figure 2 of Fry (2019) copied below). The yields of global science would be found in the periphery when the periphery can ‚Äúeffectively borrow the broker‚Äôs access to connections, knowledges and resources‚Äù (4). If the contextual factors are right, the periphery scientists should be publishing more papers and in more prestigious journals through borrowed knowledge and borrowed network position of the broker.

Panel (b) of Figure 2 from Fry (2019)

Fry has three outcome variables in her empirical analysis where the latter two are causal mechanisms that drive the first outcome.

  • publication rates weighed by journal impact factor
  • collaboration rates between non-migrant scientists and the U.S. training institutions/scientists
  • knowledge flows between the core and the periphery

I want to elaborate a little bit on the last one, how Fry measures knowledge flow.üìê One way she measures it is publication-to-publication citation rate introduced by Jaffe, Trajtenberg, and Henderson (1993) ‚ÄúGeographic Localization of Knowledge Spillovers as Evidenced by Patent Citations‚Äù. The second method, which Fry borrows from Bourdeau et al. (2016) ‚ÄúLooking Across and Looking Beyond the Knowledge Frontier: Intellectual Distance, Novelty, and Resource Allocation in Science‚Äù, quantifies the intellectual proximity between the publication outputs of the periphery and the core scientists. This is done by constructing vectors of Medical Heading Subject term frequencies for non-migrant scientists and returning trainee‚Äôs U.S. training institutions, and taking their ‚Äúcosine distance, expressed as a percentile across all sample values [where] the value of 1% reflects the least (and the closest) and 100% the greatest distance‚Äù (17). This is a clever way to avoid intepreting the cosine of 0 as the greatest (and the farthest) and the cosine of 1 as the least (and the closest).üí°

Research design

As mentioned earlier, the treatment event is the return of AITRP trainee to their home country. During the study period 1988 to 2014, there were 316 unique return events to fifteen African countries. The treated group consists of 1,657 local African scientists who are working in HIV-related field in the institutions at the time of the return of a AITRP trainee. They must have published at least one HIV-related research within three years before the AITRP trainee graduates, and over 75% of their publications during those three years must be affiliated with that institution.

These 1,657 treated African scientists are then matched with 1,657 control African scientists, who are located in countries affiliated with the AITRP but whose institution did not receive an AITRP trainee during the study period. The two groups are coarsed-exact matched by pre-treatment publication rate, pre-treatment collaboration frequencies with international or U.S.-based scientists, type of research, and career age. Figure A and Table 3 presents how balanced the treated and control groups are across these covariates and outcome variables. Their pre-treatment trends are not completely identical: according to the last two rows of Table 3 and panel (d) of Figure A, the treated and the control group are very different in their pre-treatment collaboration rates with a returnee and U.S. training institutions. I think this is where I would have liked to see a histogram to check whether there are outliers that happen to have a lot of collaborative experience with American coauthors prior to the treatment event.

Regression equation

For African scientist \(i\) at year \(t\) from the return of a trainee to her institution, Fry costructs the following model: \[ E[y_{it} \, | \, X_{it}] = \exp \Big\{ \beta_0 + \beta_1(\text{After_Return})_{it} + \beta_2(\text{After_Return})_{it} \times (\text{Return_Inst})_{i} + f(\text{Age})_{it} + \delta_t + \gamma_i \Big\}.\]

\(\text{After_Return}_{it}\) turns to 1 from 0 after the trainee returns to the home country, but \(\text{Return_Inst}_{i}\) turns to 1 from 0 when scientist \(i\) is treated. The standard errors are clustered at the level of treatment, i.e.¬†institution level.

Fry doesn‚Äôt explain why she includes \(\text{After_Return}_{it} \times \text{Return_Inst}_i\) instead of simply a dummy variable \(\text{Return_Inst}_{it}\) that switches to 1 when the trainee joins scientist \(i\)‚Äôs institution after year \(t\). In none of the results, figures, and tables does Fry mention \(\hat{\beta}_1\). My guess is that because the control scientists are still located in countries affiliated with AITRP, upon the return of a trainee to their home country the control scientists in that country will feel the effect of size \(\beta_1\) while the treated scientists will feel the effect of size \(\beta_1 + \beta_2\). This is fair if the return of a trainee is thought to have some impact on scientists across the country regardless of which specific institution the trainee returns to. If we only had \(\text{Return_Inst}_{it}\) in the regression, we wouldn‚Äôt be able to see the difference in impact on the treated and the control scientists.

Outcome of treated and control scientist before and after the return of a trainee

Maybe I was slow to understand this difference-in-difference setup, but I wish this was all explained in the text because I think the size of \(\hat{\beta_2}\) should be interpreted in the light of \(\hat{\beta_1}\). In the worst case scenario, if \(\hat{\beta}_1\) is equal or larger in size than \(\hat{\beta_2}\) and statistically significant too, the statistical significance of a positive \(\hat{\beta}_2\) wouldn‚Äôt be enough to support the second-hand brokerage theory.

Or maybe I‚Äôm misreading?

Column 4 of Table 4 shows that the scientists in the treatment group are not listed as first authors more frequently than the control group at a statistically significant level even though they are publishing more and in prestigious journals. Fry notes that this ‚Äúraises the questions on the role of the African scientists on projects, and the possibility that they are ‚Äòlong-arm‚Äô of the American labs (20)‚Äù in which ‚ÄúAfrican scientists carry out low-skilled filed work tasks to deliver samples to the United States for analysis‚Äù (27).

While I give props to Fry for taking note on this questionable aspect of ‚Äúglobal science,‚Äù I am disappointed that she doesn‚Äôt elaborate on it by and that she provides no quotes from her interviews with the trainees and African scientists to show their thoughts on this matter. I don‚Äôt know the extent to which the life science community is conscious of the predatory behavior of parachute researchers who capitalize on cheap labor and publish in prestigious journals using the samples collected in the developing world without giving credit to local scientists. But then again, maybe I‚Äôm being unfair given that political scientists who conduct field work in developing countries aren‚Äôt necessarily behaving better.

Conclusion

This paper pays a lot of attention to the causal mechanism and integrates the sociological theory in the background into the analysis of the sample at hand. Unlike most empirical papers that are only interested in identifying the treatment effect on the main outcome and scantly inquire into the causal mechanism as a filler, the causal mechanism is the essence of Fry‚Äôs paper. Using what she learned from her interviews, Fry tests additional hypotheses on the heterogeneity in treatement effect. After Table 4, the subsequent tables inspect the causal mechanism and the heterogeneity in treatement effect. For example in Table 8, Fry looks at whether trainees who took up administrative positions in their home countries are more likely to have an impact than those who assumed technical or teaching positions. This seems to have been inspired by her interviews with participants. Thus, the mixed method approach adds thickness to Fry‚Äôs research that is lacking in most empirical work in Economics.

But more than that, there is a specific reason I chose to review this paper. I wrote in the subtitle of this blog post, ‚ÄúStudying networks with good ol‚Äô regressions,‚Äù because I‚Äôm adding Fry‚Äôs paper to my collection of empirical works whose research question pertains to network effects but whose research design employs familiar tools like regressions. A case in point is Jaffe, Trajtenberg, and Henderson (1993) ‚ÄúGeographic Localization of Knowledge Spillovers as Evidenced by Patent Citations‚Äù, whose design of publication-to-publication citation rate Fry uses in her paper. The other one in the collection is Azoulay, Zivin, and Wang (2016) ‚ÄúSuperstar Extinction‚Äù, which is my favorite paper that studies a very similar phenomenon to Fry‚Äôs. I think reading these papers and Fry‚Äôs would be valuable for other researchers studying the effect of social networks.
